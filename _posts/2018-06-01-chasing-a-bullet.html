I've always been fascinated by all things space. I recently tore through Mike Massamino's book Spaceman and now I am currently reading The Intersteller Age, which is about NASA's Voyager 1 & 2 missions. My daughter delights in watching Commander Chris Hadfield's videos from the International Space Station so much that we've seen every one many many times. Since I am a certified Space Geek, it's only natural that I try to combine that with my passion for photography.

I've seen lots of photos of the International Space Station transiting both the sun and the moon, but how on earth did they do that? Could I do that? The station itself is orbiting the earth at 17,500mph, which makes photographing it like chasing a bullet. The first step was to find a tool to tell me where and when it would be. After some searching I stumbled across Transit Finder which given a lat/long and date range would give tell me exactly where and when I needed to be down to the millisecond.

Not surprisingly options are limited if you don't want to travel a few hours to make an attempt. I checked the site weekly for at least a year. Most seemingly great opportunities slipping by due to scheduling conflicts or just poor weather. I finally made my first attempt a month ago on May 1st at 6am. I rolled out of bed at 4:30 and drove 45 minutes south to see if I could capture the ISS just after sunrise. Once everything was setup I used an iPhone app called SkyView Lite to track the progress of both the station and the Moon, and everything looked perfect. I was ready.

I checked my phone a few more times, and when I got about two minutes out I just sat there, staring through the camera's eye piece watching as hard as I could. Waiting. Waiting. Waiting. Nothing. Checked the app again and the ISS had blown right past, I have no idea if it was close, I never even spotted it. I was dissapointed, but determined to figure it out. When I got home I realized that the ISS was illuminated and appeared as a white spot reflecting sunlight, and I was looking for the distinct black silhouette making the crossing. Okay, rookie mistake. I made a note and went back to checking Transit Finder every few days.

Finally two weeks ago I saw a transit happening just after the full moon, 6 minutes after midnight on a Thursday night. Looking closer I could get on the center line only travelling about 20 minutes from home, and thats including three backup sites incase of who knows what. I picked a farm road off the beaten path where I could setup and not be hassled by anyone. I had the time, I had the place, now the only x-factor was the weather.
